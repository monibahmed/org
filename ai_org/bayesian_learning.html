2 Bayes Rule and Probability Review

Bayes' theorem describes the probability of an event, based on prior knowledge of conditions that might be related to an event. One application of Bayes Theorem is Bayesian inference.

In probability theory, an event is a set of outcomes of an experiment (a subset of the sample space) to which a probability is assigned. A single outcome may be an element of many different events and different events may not be equally likely. An event defines a complementary event (the event not occuring), and together these define a Bernouli trial (did the event occur or not?).

The sample space is finite, and any subset sample space is an event. However this does not work in cases where the sample space is infinite.

Defining all subsets of the sample space as events works well when there are only finitely many outcomes, but gives problems when the sample space is infinite.

For the standard tools of probability theory, such as joint and conditional probabilities, to work, it is necessary to use σ-Algebra (a familt closed under complementation and countable unions of its members).

Bayes' theorem is stated mathematically as the following equation: \[P(A|B) = \frac{P(B|A)P(A)}{P(B)}\]

\(P(A|B)\) is a conditional probability, the likelihood of event A occuring given the B is true.

\(P(B|A)\) is a conditional probability, the likelihood of event B occuring given the A is true.

\(P(A)\) and \(P(B)\) are the probabilities of observing A and B independently of each other (marginal probability).

2.1 Conditional Probability

Conditional probability is a measure of the probability of an event given that (by assumption, presumption, assertion or evidence) another event has occured.

The probability \(P(A|B)\) may or may nnot be equal to \(P(A)\). If \(P(A|B) = P(A)\), then the events A and B are said to be independent. In other words, having knowledge about either event does not change our knowledge about the other event.

The probability \(P(A|B)\) is not equal to \(P(B|A)\). This relates to sensitivty and specificity.

2.2 Sensitivity and Specificity

Sensitivity - probability of correct positive detection Specificity - probability of correct negative detection

Sensitivity is the extent which true positives are not missed or overlooked.

Terminology:

Terminology Description
P Num of. Positive samples
N Num of. Negative samples
TP Num of. Positive hit samples (got right)
TN Num of. Negative hit samples (got right)
FP Num of. False Positives (false alarms)
FN Num of. False Negatives (misses)
   

Equations:

Equation Description Description
\(TPR = \frac{TP}{TP+FN}\) Hit Rate, Sensitivity hits
\(TNR = \frac{TN}{TN+FP}\) Specificity misses
\(PPV = \frac{TP}{TP+FP}\) Precision  
\(NPV = \frac{TN}{TN+FN}\)
